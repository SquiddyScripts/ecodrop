Gravitational Energy StorageΓÇôRegeneration Systems for Existing Infrastructure

A measured weight-drop prototype, four candidate architectures, and their comparative simulation ΓÇö recorded with full iteration history
Amaan S. Khan ┬╖ Aiman Ullah ┬╖ Kavin Paturu Muralikrishnan
Chantilly High School, Virginia ┬╖ 2024 ΓÇô present
Abstract

This record evaluates the energy efficiency of four small-scale gravitational storageΓÇôregeneration concepts ΓÇö buoyant displacement, electromagnetic assist (quasi-Halbach tubular linear machine), dual-weight counterbalance, and variable counterweight ΓÇö using MATLAB simulation with strict end-to-end energy bookkeeping, calibrated against a physically built and instrumented generation prototype. Each design is modeled under identical baseline conditions (50 kg effective mass, 3 m travel) and assessed by electrical energy generated per drop, electrical energy required for lift, discharge efficiency, and per-channel loss attribution. The variable-counterweight architecture attains the highest simulated discharge efficiency (77.0 ┬▒ 0.5 %); one-way ANOVA establishes architecture as a significant factor (F(3,36) = 6.10, p < 0.001, ╬╖┬▓ = 0.337). The complete component iteration history ΓÇö including rejected mechanisms ΓÇö is preserved here and in the source repository.

Problem statement and prior art

Intermittent renewable generation must be decoupled from consumption, and the dominant storage answer ΓÇö electrochemical batteries ΓÇö carries energy-intensive mining, toxic disposal, and per-cycle chemical degradation. Gravity storage stores energy in the position of a mass: mechanically simple, chemically inert, and effectively unlimited in cycle life. At grid scale it already works ΓÇö pumped hydro achieves 70ΓÇô85 % round-trip efficiency[5], and dry-mass systems from Energy Vault and Gravitricity, underground shaft concepts[3], and piston designs[2,6] are commercial or near-commercial.

Lithium-ion round-trip efficiency is high (85ΓÇô92 %) ΓÇö the argument against it here is not efficiency but chemistry: extraction, degradation, and disposal.

The unsolved case is small scale. Grid-scale gravity plants are location-bound and capital-heavy; the literature identifies buildings themselves as the untapped medium ΓÇö Lift Energy Storage Technology proposes elevator-borne mass transport in high-rises[4], and elevator regenerative drives with ultracapacitor buffering are an active research area[7,8,9]. This project asks the question those threads point toward but do not answer:

Research question ┬╖ research plan, 2025ΓÇô26

What viable gravitational storageΓÇôregeneration systems can be implemented in the real world ΓÇö inside existing infrastructure ΓÇö and which architecture recovers the largest fraction of the energy invested in lifting?

The program proceeded in the only defensible order: first build and measure a physical generation prototype to ground every loss model in instrument data (┬º2); then design candidate architectures with full iteration discipline (┬º3); then compare them under identical simulated conditions with statistical inference (┬º4ΓÇô┬º5).

The generation prototype MEASURED

System architecture

The prototype converts gravitational potential energy to electrical energy through a five-stage mechanical chain: barbell masses tied to polypropylene rope, redirected by a pulley, wound on a drum, geared up, and delivered to a DC machine operating as a generator. A ~9-foot wooden scaffold frame carries the drop path; a weight box constrains lateral sway.

Lateral swaying during early drop trials measurably reduced energy transfer to the drum ΓÇö the weight-box enclosure was itself an iteration response, not part of the first design.

Component iteration record

The logbook records every configuration tried, its failure mode, and the engineering response. The record below is condensed but complete ΓÇö nothing that failed is omitted, because the failures carry the design rationale.

Gearbox ΓÇö four generations

Iteration 1Compound gearbox, Vex/Lego componentsSUPERSEDED~$15
A ~200-tooth gear meshing an ~18-tooth pinion at small module size. Failure modeGear slippage under load; meshing gaps produced dropouts in the generation output. ResponseIncrease tooth thickness and module; move to a stackable architecture with proper bearings.
Iteration 2Compound stackable spur gearboxSUPERSEDED~$10
Laser-cut wooden structure, progressive RPM amplification across stages, ball bearings at key shafts. LimitationFunctioned, but spur-contact losses across multiple stages capped efficiency below target.
Iteration 3Planetary gearboxREJECTED~$18
Evaluated for its torque-and-speed amplification in a compact stack (the drill-gearbox architecture). Why rejectedHigh frictional losses inherent to the geometry; prototype-scale build complexity; net gain did not justify it.
Iteration 4Double-helix spur gearboxFINAL~$13
Double-helix spur gears engineered for high speed and load; polycarbonate gear material. Key changesRound dowel shafts (torque fluctuation from non-circular sections) ΓåÆ aluminum hex shafts; standard ball bearings ΓåÆ angular-contact bearings (the load has both radial and axial components); dual hinge shaft collars restrict lateral gear movement under load. Teflon washers and polycarbΓÇônylon composite under evaluation.

Mechanical connections

Custom 3D-printed PLA couplers (Γëê$0.50) joined spool-to-gearbox and gearbox-to-machine in the first build. Slight axial misalignment multiplied torque demand under load; couplers vibrated and failed. The industry-derived response replaced couplers entirely: a chainΓÇôsprocket stage (FRC robotics practice for high-load transmission) at spool-to-gearbox, and a 1:1 bevel-gear right-angle stage at gearbox-to-machine ΓÇö tolerant of misalignment and tunable in ratio.

The bevel stage adds friction but buys a necessary 90┬░ redirection of the rotational axis ΓÇö an accepted, documented trade.

Rope winding ΓÇö spool to hoist drum

Iteration 1Modified weed-whacker spoolSUPERSEDED~$12.50
Failure modeA trimmer spool stores line; it does not control unwinding. Under dynamic load: tangling, inconsistent tension, no bearings. ResponseResearch into industrial lifting hardware identified the distinction that matters ΓÇö a hoist drum carries cleats, grooves matched to the rope profile that enforce a standard winding path.
Iteration 2Cleated hoist drum (Onshape) + rope guideFINAL
A cleat structure designed in Onshape and fitted over the existing spool geometry, plus an industrial-style rope guide. Tangling eliminated in both winding directions. Re-printed and refined again in the 2026 rebuild.

Pulley system and weight stabilization

The block-and-tackle used for mechanical advantage introduced one friction point per sheave; the final configuration simplifies to a single redirecting pulley (Γëê$27.50 including rigging) with grease at the rope contact. Drop trials exposed lateral sway that bled energy from the descent; the response was a weight-box enclosure constraining the mass to vertical translation.

Machine selection

The first generator ΓÇö a brushed DC unit (Γëê$17.25) ΓÇö lost output to brush friction, brush wear, and high internal resistance. Selection of its replacement was done with a consulted motor engineer against the application's defining constraint: gravitational drop speeds are low, so the machine must produce usable voltage at low shaft speed ΓÇö high stall torque, low Kv, minimal internal resistance. No inrunner met the combined requirement; the survey settled on an outrunner.

Measured characterization

Drop trials at four mass levels (10, 15, 20, 25 kg; three trials each; Γëê2.5 m drop) were instrumented with a multimeter. Efficiency rises with mass because fixed friction terms amortize over a larger gravitational input ΓÇö peak 28.7 % end-to-end at 25 kg (8.23 V mean, Γëê45 W average electrical output).

Recorded drop height varies across primary documents (2 m plan / 2.5 m analysis / 2.7 m logbook) ΓÇö the frame was rebuilt more than once. Flagged in ┬º10, not silently reconciled.

Four storageΓÇôregeneration architectures SIMULATED IN DESIGN

With the generation chain validated, the design space forked: where in the built world do masses already rise and fall, and which lifting mechanism wastes the least of what it stores? Four architectures were carried to full design. Their iteration records ΓÇö including the concepts each one killed ΓÇö follow. A fifth concept, a street-lamp gravity battery, was designed and dropped before simulation.

The street-lamp branch survives only in correspondence (documents/notes) ΓÇö an honest artifact of scope narrowing in fall 2025.

Buoyancy system ΓÇö storage

Energy is stored by lifting the mass with Archimedes' force rather than a motor: flood a chamber, and a flotation body strapped to the mass rises; drain it, and the mass descends through the generation chain. The lift force follows Eq. (5) below.

Iteration 1Bottom-pump chamber, pneumatic releaseREJECTED
Why rejectedPneumatically actuated drain ports added cost and complexity disproportionate to function.
Iteration 2Three-chamber configurationSUPERSEDED
Two side reservoirs pump into a central travel chamber; trapdoor return. LimitationFunctional but inefficient; trapdoor control complexity.
Iteration 3Dual-layer chamberFINAL DESIGN
Upper reservoir floods an outer chamber; water enters the inner travel chamber only through a bottom gap, so buoyant force develops without wetting rope or mass ΓÇö wet rope raises friction sharply and wood swells. Three in-path turbines harvest the falling water; a check-valved vertical pump recharges the upper reservoir. Sub-problem solvedBuoyant lift puts the rope in compression (it can only carry tension) ΓÇö a high-torque idler between pulley and drum maintains active tension through the lift phase.

Halbach tubular linear machine ΓÇö storage, electromagnetic

The electromagnetic branch asks whether the lifting chain can be removed entirely: no rope, no drum, no gearbox on the way up. Its iteration record is the project's longest chain of rejected concepts.

Iteration 1Fixed electromagnet pair, top and bottomREJECTED
Why rejectedField strength must scale with the full travel column ΓÇö electromagnet size and cost scale prohibitively, and a static field cannot adapt to variable load.
Iteration 2Weight-as-electromagnet (soft-iron core, wound)REJECTED
Why rejectedManufacturing a massive wound electromagnet is exactly the cost structure commercial gravity storage avoids ΓÇö Energy Vault's masses are compacted waste material for a reason.
Iteration 3Flat linear motorREJECTED
Linear motors convert electrical energy directly to translation (maglev propulsion), skipping the rotary-to-linear stage of a linear actuator. Why rejectedPlanar geometry leaks flux and cannot develop the required thrust density for vertical mass lifting.
Iteration 4Tubular linear machine, T-shaped quasi-Halbach magnetizationFINAL DESIGN ┬╖ ACTIVE
Cylindrical Halbach arrays concentrate flux toward the bore (Γëê1.2 T), and finite-element results in the literature show T-shaped and trapezoidal quasi-Halbach magnet structures superior to rectangular PMs in thrust force, power density, and cogging[1]. Final architecture: four tubular linear machines on the mass's shell; inward-pointing T-shaped Halbach rails; six-phase stacked coil arrangement; contact-free travel. StatusThe current research direction (┬º8); lowest simulated efficiency in ┬º5, retained for its controllability and mechanical simplicity.
Theoretical extension ΓÇö recorded and self-corrected ┬╖ logbook, Part III

A descent concept combining gravitational and eddy-current forces was explored on paper ΓÇö cutting translator excitation at top-of-stroke so the falling conductor generates through the Halbach field. The logbook itself notes the correction: the eddy interaction brakes descent rather than accelerating it, and no configuration returns more than was stored. Retained as a documented exploration, marked REJECTED.

Dual-weight system ΓÇö regenerative

Traction elevators already contain the mechanism: cab and counterweight coupled over a sheave, counterweight sized near 50 % of rated cab load. The dual-weight system applies the machine-room topology directly ΓÇö two hoist drums on a common shaft, ropes wound in opposition, one BLDC machine generating on both directions of travel. Two variants are recorded: an elevator-integration model (retrofit generation module, polarity-switched on cab direction ΓÇö the commercial analogue is the regenerative drive[7,9]) and the free-standing simulation model used in ┬º5.

Key insight from the logbook: with matched masses, the descending weight lifts the ascending one ΓÇö the machine pays only imbalance plus friction. That observation is the seed of ┬º3.4.

Variable counterweight ΓÇö regenerative, highest-performing

The variable counterweight was not conceived as a battery. It emerged from elevator counterweight sizing research: real counterweights are optimized per building ΓÇö height, traffic pattern, rated load ΓÇö not fixed at 50 %. If a counterweight can be optimized statically, it can be matched dynamically: sensors read the primary load, and modular masses deploy or retract to bring net imbalance toward zero, so the machine supplies only friction and residual imbalance on every ascent.

The mechanism has two mass populations: a primary cabled mass on the generation chain, and modular magnetic barbell masses ΓÇö each wound with electromagnet coils for docking, each carrying guide-roller generators that charge onboard supercapacitors during module descent, discharged to the building bus at the docking checkpoint. Secondary recovery on top of primary generation.

Module electronics (supercapacitor bank, low-dropout regulator) are engineered on paper only ΓÇö the whole architecture is simulated + CAD, and ┬º10 lists it among unbuilt claims.

Mathematical model and simulation protocol

All four architectures share one physics core; each contributes a system-specific loss channel on top. Bookkeeping is end-to-end and resolved per timestep ΓÇö loss terms are functions of instantaneous velocity, not static averages applied after the fact.

$$E_g \;=\; m\,g\,\Delta h$$
$$\mathrm{DE} \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\int_{0}^{t_d} V(t)\,I(t)\,\mathrm{d}t}{m\,g\,\Delta h}\times 100\,\%$$
$$J_{\mathrm{eff}}\,\dot{\omega} \;=\; \frac{T_{\mathrm{load}}}{N}\;-\;T_{\mathrm{fric}}(\omega)\;-\;K_t\,I,\qquad J_{\mathrm{eff}} \;=\; J_m+\sum_i \frac{J_i}{\eta_i\,N_i^{2}}$$
$$V \;=\; K_e\,\omega_m-I\,R_m,\qquad K_t \;=\; \frac{60}{2\pi\,K_v}\;\approx\;0.0405\ \mathrm{N\,m\,A^{-1}}$$
$$F_b \;=\; \rho_{\mathrm{w}}\,V_{\mathrm{disp}}\,g \;-\; m g \;-\; F_{\mathrm{drag}}(v)$$

(1) stored energy per cycle ΓÇö 1 471.5 J at the 50 kg / 3 m baseline, identical across systems ┬╖ (2) discharge efficiency, the primary metric (also the metric used to rate pumped hydro and Li-ion, enabling ┬º5's benchmark comparison) ┬╖ (3) rotational dynamics integrated by ode45, inertias reflected through gear stages ┬╖ (4) machine model of the SK3-5065 (Table 1) ┬╖ (5) net buoyant lift in the ┬º3.1 chamber.

Protocol

  1. Common baseline. 50 kg effective mass, 3 m travel, identical drum, gear ratio, rope, and machine model across all four systems; only the architecture differs.
  2. Calibration. Friction coefficients and machine efficiency fitted to the ┬º2.4 multimeter data before any comparison was run.
  3. System-specific losses. Variable CW: mass-transfer energy cost; dual weight: drum friction and rope routing; buoyancy: fluid drag and pump work; Halbach: coil and braking resistance.
  4. Replication. 10 runs per system with machine efficiency perturbed ┬▒2 % and friction ┬▒5 %, representing run-to-run uncertainty.
  5. Inference. One-way ANOVA across systems on discharge efficiency and net energy per cycle; effect size by ╬╖┬▓.
  6. Sensitivity. All friction parameters swept ┬▒20 %; ranking invariance checked.

Interpretation. Discharge efficiency is set by how much net work the machine must do against gravity per cycle. The variable counterweight drives net imbalance toward zero and therefore reduces its loss budget to drivetrain friction (305 J/cycle); the buoyancy and Halbach systems pay a full lift-energy toll through pumping and coil losses respectively (1 680 and 3 100 J/cycle). The gap compounds linearly with cycling.

Applications and current direction IN DESIGN

Elevator retrofit. The regenerative pair (┬º3.3, ┬º3.4) maps onto traction-elevator infrastructure: cab and counterweight already exist, modular masses stage at floor landings, and regeneration occurs on every trip rather than only under favorable load as in current commercial drives[7,9]. Representative elevator dimensional analysis toward this application was performed during summer work at Virginia Tech (┬º10, item 2).

Water infrastructure. The buoyancy system (┬º3.1) targets coastal facilities, treatment plants, and water towers, where the working fluid and vertical plumbing already exist.

Electromagnetic direction. Active design work continues on the quasi-Halbach tubular machine (┬º3.2) ΓÇö the architecture with the fewest moving parts and full position control ΓÇö and on the concept manuscript (documents/concept-paper/, draft with placeholder values, flagged in-source). Off-grid and backup deployments, where mechanical maintainability beats battery logistics, are the long-term frame.

Prior art boundary, kept explicit: linear-machine dry gravity storage exists at industrial scale in the literature ΓÇö the contribution pursued here is the student-scale comparative architecture study, not a novelty claim on linear machines.

Provenance, limitations, and open questions

Every quantitative claim on this page carries one of four evidence classes (front matter). Three limitations govern interpretation, and the repository's PROVENANCE.md maintains the complete map.

  1. The ┬º5 numbers are simulations. None of the four architectures exists as a full physical machine; loss models are calibrated to ┬º2.4 but not validated against an architecture-scale build. An earlier code generation (January 2026, Pipeline B) reported higher absolute values ΓÇö 89.7 % for the variable counterweight ΓÇö before the calibrated rebuild; both codebases and outputs are preserved, and the presentation-script draft in which the team flagged its own electromagnetic-model error is retained verbatim.
  2. Attribution gaps are flagged, not filled. The summer-2025 internship host and the mentor "Dr. Jim" are under-documented in primary sources; the drop height appears as 2 / 2.5 / 2.7 m across documents; the origin of two 3D-print STL sets is unconfirmed.
  3. Placements are not claimed. The ┬º9 record documents participation; award outcomes are not stated in the archived files and are therefore not stated here.
