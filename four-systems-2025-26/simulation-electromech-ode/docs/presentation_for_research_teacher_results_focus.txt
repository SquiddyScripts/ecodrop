---- 1. SIMULATION ----

What the simulation tells us
• The simulation tells us how much electrical energy each system would put in and get out over one full cycle, under the same height and mass and the same type of motion. So we get a fair comparison: same "script," different designs.
• It tells us that the four designs do not perform the same: we get different round-trip efficiencies and different net energies. The simulation produced the numbers we then tested with ANOVA and reported in the conclusion.
• It tells us that all systems lose energy on balance (negative net energy per cycle), which is what we expect when there are losses—so the results are thermodynamically plausible.

Why we use a set curve instead of F = ma (prescribed motion)
• If we solved F = ma for each system, each design would have different equations and different motion—different speeds at different times. Then we couldn't compare "at the same point" because the four systems wouldn't be at the same point at the same time; they'd each have their own trajectory.
• So instead we fix the motion first: we choose one curve for "how the thing moves" over the cycle—e.g. how fast it goes down, how fast it goes up, and for how long. That's the same for all four in spirit (same height, same mass, same kind of smooth motion). So at every time point we're comparing the same input: "here is the position and speed; now how much power does each design put in or get out?"
• That way we compare at every point: same motion in, different power out (because each design converts that motion to electricity differently—different motors, pumps, counterweights, etc.). So the only thing that changes between systems is the design, not the motion. That's why we use a set curve: so the comparison is fair and we can say "under the same motion, this design gives this efficiency and net energy."


---- 2. GRAPHS ----

Overall: what each dashboard tells us (brief)
• Buoyancy dashboard: Shows one full cycle—lift, water phase, drop. It tells us when we're putting power in (motor + pump) vs when we're getting it back (generator on drop), and how the water levels and cumulative energy evolve. So we see why this system has the efficiency it does: long lift and pump cost, then one drop phase for payback.
• Dual-weight dashboard: Shows cab and counterweight moving in opposite directions over one cycle. It tells us when we're generating (descent) vs consuming (ascent) and how mechanical power becomes electrical. So we see the elevator-style balance and where the losses are.
• Electromagnetic dashboard: Shows the weight moving up (lift), hold, then down (drop) with linear motors and hoist. It tells us consumption during lift and hold and regeneration during drop. So we see the cost of the linear motors and hoist and why this system ends up with lower efficiency in our comparison.
• Variable counterweight dashboard: Shows the mass going down (heavy), then up (lighter) with modules changing over the cycle. It tells us how much we generate when heavy and how little we consume when lifting light, plus the cumulative energy building to a net closer to zero. So we see why this design gets the best efficiency in our comparison.

What the comparison figure (panel) tells us
This is the figure that brings all four systems together (bar charts + ANOVA + conclusion panel).
• Panel A — Round-trip efficiency bar chart: Average efficiency per system and spread (error bars); which system recovers the most per unit put in. Variable counterweight highest; Electromagnetic lowest.
• Panel B — Net energy per cycle bar chart: How much each system gets out minus puts in per cycle (all negative); which loses the least. Variable counterweight best.
• Panel C — ANOVA table: System type significantly affects both metrics (small p); effect size meaningful. Differences are not just noise.
• Panel D — Conclusion panel: Best system and full ranking in one place.


---- 3. CONCLUSIONS ----

• Main finding: Under the conditions we used (3 m height, 50 kg effective mass), the Variable counterweight system performed best: highest round-trip efficiency (about 90%) and the best net energy recovery. So among these four designs, that one gives back the most electricity per unit we put in and loses the least per cycle.

• Ranking: The systems are ordered Variable counterweight > Dual-weight elevator > Buoyancy > Electromagnetic (Halbach). So the conclusion tells us that system type clearly affects performance—it’s not a tie, and the ranking is consistent.

• Statistical support: The tests tell us the differences between the four systems are unlikely to be due to chance (p < 0.001 for both efficiency and net energy). Effect size (eta-squared ~0.33) tells us that which system you use explains a substantial share of the variation in the results. So we can say the ranking is statistically meaningful.

• Recommendation: For maximum round-trip efficiency and best net recovery under these test conditions, the Variable counterweight is the preferred option among the four. The conclusion also says that in practice you’d still consider complexity, cost, and scalability.

• Limitations: The conclusion states that these are simplified models with parameter uncertainty, and that real prototypes or higher-fidelity simulation would strengthen the conclusions. So we’re not overclaiming—we’re saying what the comparison shows under these assumptions.

---- OPTIONAL: WHAT EACH DASHBOARD PLOT TELLS US (if asked about a specific plot) ----


--- BUOYANCY GRAVITY BATTERY ---

• Buoyant weight height: Tells us how high the weight is over the cycle and when we’re in the lift vs drop part—so we see the stroke and timing of the cycle.

• Weight velocity: Tells us how fast and in which direction the weight moves at each time—so we see when we’re charging (up) vs discharging (down) and how the speed varies.

• Water levels (chamber and reservoir): Tells us how the water is moved between chamber and reservoir over time—so we see the hydraulic mechanism that makes the buoyancy system work and when the pump is active.

• Motor and pump (consumption): Tells us how much electricity we’re using to lift the weight and run the pump—so we see the “cost” of the charge phase.

• Generator output (drop phase): Tells us how much electricity we get back when the weight drops—so we see the “payback” of the discharge phase.

• Charge vs discharge power: Tells us when we’re putting power in vs taking it out and how big those rates are—so we see the balance of the cycle in one view.

• Cumulative energy: Tells us how much we’ve put in and how much we’ve got out by any time in the cycle—so we see total consumed, total generated, and net building up over time. The final values are what we use for efficiency and net energy.

• Cycle energy split (pie): Tells us in one picture how much of the cycle’s energy was consumed vs generated—so we see at a glance whether this system is close to break-even or losing a lot.

• Weight phase portrait (h vs v): Tells us how velocity and height relate over the cycle (the loop)—so we see that the motion is one consistent cycle with no impossible behavior.

• Cab and counterweight height: Tells us that the cab and counterweight move in opposite directions—so we see the elevator-style kinematics and the stroke.

• Cab and counterweight velocity: Tells us their speeds match in magnitude but are opposite in sign—so we see they’re coupled and when we’re in descent vs ascent.

• Mechanical and electrical power: Tells us how much power is at the drum (mechanical) and after conversion (electrical)—so we see when we’re generating vs consuming and how much is lost in conversion.

• Regeneration vs consumption: Tells us when we get energy back (descent) vs when we put it in (ascent) and the relative size of those phases—so we see the split of the cycle.

• Regeneration efficiency (descent phase): Tells us how well we convert motion to electricity during the drop—so we see that we don’t get 100% back even during generation.

• Motor / generator current: Tells us when the machine is drawing current (motoring) vs supplying it (generating)—so we see the same device doing both jobs.

• Cumulative energy: Tells us total generated, consumed, and net over the cycle—so we see the numbers that feed into round-trip efficiency and net energy.

• Cycle energy split (pie): Tells us the ratio of regenerated to consumed in one cycle—so we see this system’s efficiency at a glance.

---- 6. IF ASKED ABOUT METHODS (SHORT ANSWERS) ----

• How did you simulate? We prescribed the motion for each system (same height and mass, fixed phase lengths) and computed power in and out, then integrated to get energy. So we get round-trip efficiency and net energy per cycle. We ran each system 10 times with slightly different efficiency parameters to get replicates.

• How did you compare? We used one-way ANOVA on efficiency and on net energy to test whether the four systems differ; we reported F, p-value, and eta-squared. The bar charts show mean and standard deviation per system.

• Why prescribed motion? So all four systems see the same type of cycle and we only change the design. That keeps the comparison fair.
